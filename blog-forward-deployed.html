So we don't try. When a team starts with Marzy, a Mulholland engineer embeds with them — watches how the work actually moves, where it gets stuck, and which steps only look like they need a human. That week of context is worth more than a month of specs.

The map comes before the automation

Most back-office work is undocumented by definition — it lives in the habits of the person who does it. The first job is to make that visible: the inputs, the exceptions, the judgment calls. Once the map exists, the automation is almost obvious.

  • Week 0 — the engineer embeds and maps how work moves.
  • Weeks 1–2 — you flag what's safe to automate.
  • Weeks 3–4 — agents ship behind your approval.

Trust is built in person

Handing routine work to an agent is a leap. It's a smaller leap when the person who built the automation sat next to you while you did the work by hand. Forward-deploying isn't just efficient — it's how trust gets earned early.

It compounds

The first automation is the hardest. After that, each one reuses the map, the connectors, and the trust already established. Margins keep improving as more of the work moves behind approval — and eventually runs on its own.
